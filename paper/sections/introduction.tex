\section{Introduction}
\label{intro}

The digital economy is undergoing a phase transition from human-centric commerce to agent-centric ecosystems. Platforms such as Amazon and Moltbook already host autonomous agents that execute pricing decisions, inventory updates, and purchase bids at speeds impossible for human operators. Current estimates place the scale of LLM-driven agent transactions above one million per day on major marketplaces, and this number is growing rapidly. While individual agents may be rational optimizers in isolation, their collective interaction introduces severe systemic risks. Flash crashes, liquidity crises, and deceptive equilibria can emerge rapidly when thousands of unaligned agents interact under partial observability \citep{kirilenko2017flash,farmer2009economy}.

To ensure the safety of future economic systems, we must extend the definition of \textit{AI Alignment} beyond chat safety to \textbf{Economic Alignment}. We define an economically aligned agent as one that (1) contributes to smooth, stable market dynamics rather than chaotic volatility, and (2) protects the welfare of human participants against exploitation or fraud. Crucially, economic alignment is \emph{orthogonal} to general reasoning capability: a state-of-the-art LLM can solve complex logic puzzles while simultaneously driving a market into collapse through locally rational but globally destructive pricing decisions. Standard alignment approaches targeting factuality, helpfulness, and harmlessness do not capture this property \citep{ouyang2022training}.

Real-world marketplaces impose \textbf{Search Friction} on all participants. A buyer on Amazon compares a handful of listings despite thousands being available; an LLM seller agent has a finite context window that bounds how many competitor prices it can observe per decision cycle. We model this formally as a token budget $\tau$ that restricts each agent's observable subset of the global market state. This partial observability is the mechanism by which local optimization leads to global failure: each agent acts rationally on its incomplete view, but the aggregate behavior produces a cascade.

We identify two distinct failure modes that arise from the interaction of partial observability, LLM myopia, and the cheapness of LLM-generated identities. The first is \textbf{Algorithmic Instability} (``The Crash''): in B2C markets, firms engage in a recursive undercutting race until prices fall below unit cost, triggering a wave of bankruptcies and market collapse---an LLM-native analog of the 2010 Flash Crash. The second is \textbf{Sybil Deception} (``The Lemon Market''): in C2C markets, a single deceptive principal can cheaply operate $K$ seller identities with independent reputations. When one identity's reputation degrades from repeated fraud, the principal retires it and activates a fresh identity, perpetually resetting the trust signal that buyers rely on. This is Akerlof's market for lemons \citep{akerlof1970market}, amplified by the Sybil attack \citep{douceur2002sybil}.

In this work, we introduce \textbf{Agent Bazaar}, a dynamic testbed for evaluating Economic Alignment across both failure modes (Figure~\ref{fig:teaser}). Our contributions are three-fold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A Safety Benchmark for Agent Economies:} We provide a high-fidelity simulator for both B2C (Amazon-style) and C2C (eBay-style) markets, explicitly modeling non-stationarity via supply/demand shocks, information asymmetry, and Sybil identity cycling.
    \item \textbf{Identification and Formalization of Failure Modes:} We formally characterize and empirically demonstrate two emergent failure modes---Algorithmic Instability (The Crash) and Sybil Deception (The Lemon Market)---in five open-source model families operating under standard prompting.
    \item \textbf{Training for Economic Safety:} We show that standard prompting is insufficient to achieve alignment. Instead, finetuning agents on curated market trajectories---creating \textbf{Stabilizing Firms} and \textbf{Skeptical Guardians}---is necessary to restore equilibrium and protect consumer surplus in non-stationary environments.
\end{enumerate}
