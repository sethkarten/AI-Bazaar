\subsection{Experiment 1: The Crash}
\label{results:crash}
We run the $(k, \mathrm{dlc})$ ablation for three models: Gemini~3 Flash Preview,
Claude Sonnet~4.6, and GPT~5.4. The three models exhibit qualitatively distinct
baseline behaviors and require different intervention levels to achieve stability,
revealing that crash susceptibility is a property of the model's pricing strategy as
much as the market structure.\seth{economic harness vs model's capability vs env (other agents)?}

\textbf{Three qualitatively different baseline behaviors.}
The $k=0$ baseline at $\mathrm{dlc}=3$ differs significantly across models
(Figures~\ref{fig:exp1_heatmap_gemini}--\ref{fig:exp1_heatmap_gpt}).
Gemini~3 Flash Preview exhibits the two-stage crash: competitive firms
undercut each other below unit cost, four of five go bankrupt ($b_r = 0.80$), and the
surviving monopolist inflates prices to $\bar{p}/c = 3.70$ ($\sigma = 1.15$).
GPT~5.4 produces the most severe failure --- total market death, with all five firms
going bankrupt ($b_r = 1.00$, $\bar{p}/c = 0.00$) and no surviving monopolist at all.
Claude Sonnet~4.6, by contrast, does not crash at baseline: competitive firms
self-organize to a viable equilibrium ($b_r = 0.00$, $\bar{p}/c = 2.00$,
$\sigma = 0.09$), achieving the stability criterion without any intervention. \seth{but barely make any money}

\textbf{Phase structure and minimum intervention $k^*$.}
Figure~\ref{fig:exp1_phase_gemini} (Gemini), \ref{fig:exp1_phase_sonnet} (Sonnet),
and \ref{fig:exp1_phase_gpt} (GPT) show the stability phase maps and $k^*(\mathrm{dlc})$
for each model. The results differ substantially.

- note why we are only viewing k 0 1 3 5 and dlc 1 3 (check appendix for full data)

For Gemini~3 Flash Preview, stabilizing firms are effective but costly: $k^* = 1$ at
$\mathrm{dlc}=1$ (stability margin $+0.03$), rising to $k^* = 4$ at $\mathrm{dlc}=3$
(margin $+0.17$), with no stable configuration at $\mathrm{dlc}=5$. The boundary runs diagonally through the grid, with seven of fifteen tested cells
achieving stability.

- For Claude Sonnet~4.6, $k^* = 0$ at $\mathrm{dlc}=3$ --- the market is stable with
no intervention --- and $k^* = 1$ at $\mathrm{dlc}=1$. Agents adopted much more conservative strategies, purchasing small amounts of supply at a time as opposed to large quantities like Gemini and GPT. 
- All tested configurations at $\mathrm{dlc} \leq 3$ with $k \leq 3$ are stable, with
margins between $+0.17$ and $+0.50$.
- The one anomalous result is $k=5$, $\mathrm{dlc}=3$, which collapses (margin $-0.10$). This suggests that this setting reverts to the 5 competitive firms setting, forcing firms into attrition. 

For GPT~5.4, stability is only achievable at $\mathrm{dlc}=1$, where $k^* = 1$
(margins $+0.30$--$+0.50$ for $k \in \{1,3,5\}$). At $\mathrm{dlc}=3$, no tested
$k$ achieves stability, and the baseline margin is $-1.00$ (the minimum possible).

\textbf{Discovery limit is the hardest constraint.}
Across all three models, stability at $\mathrm{dlc}=5$ is either unachievable or
untested. For Gemini and GPT, the phase map shows that higher dlc requires strictly
more stabilizing firms and eventually crosses outside the achievable range. This
establishes that reducing consumer search friction increases crash risk regardless of
model, and that no tested level of stabilizing-firm intervention can compensate for
fully competitive consumer search at $\mathrm{dlc}=5$.

\textbf{Prices in stable regimes are near cost, not at extremes.}
Across all three models, stable configurations converge to prices modestly above unit
cost. For Gemini at stable cells, $\bar{p}/c \in [1.45, 1.61]$. For Sonnet,
$\bar{p}/c \in [1.58, 2.00]$ across stable cells. For GPT at $\mathrm{dlc}=1$,
$\bar{p}/c \in [1.82, 2.82]$, somewhat higher, consistent with its more aggressive
pricing under low competition. In all cases prices remain well below monopolist
levels, suggesting that the stabilizing firm's price anchor prevents both the
below-cost spiral and the post-collapse gouging dynamic seen in the GPT baseline.
